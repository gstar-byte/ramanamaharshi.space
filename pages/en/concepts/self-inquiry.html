🔍 Self-Inquiry (Atma Vichara)

The Core Practice of Ramana Maharshi's Teaching

What is Self-Inquiry?

Self-Inquiry (Atma Vichara) is the fundamental teaching of Sri Ramana Maharshi. It is not a technique or a method, but a direct path to discover your true nature through the question "Who am I?"

Unlike other spiritual practices that involve concentration, visualization, or devotion, Self-Inquiry is the most direct method because it goes straight to the "I"-thought itself - the root of all other thoughts.

The Method

Ramana Maharshi taught that when thoughts arise, instead of following them, one should ask:

"To whom does this thought arise?"

The answer will be: "To me."

Then ask: "Who am I?"

This investigation should not be done verbally or mentally as a thinking process, but as a deep inward attention. The question "Who am I?" is not meant to get an answer, but to trace the "I"-thought to its source.

Key Instructions from Ramana Maharshi

"The mind will initially resist this investigation and wander here and there. But with patient practice, whenever a thought arises, you must question: 'To whom does this thought come?' The answer is 'To me.' Then inquire 'Who am I?'

Persistently dive inward, tracing the 'I'-thought to its source. The mind will eventually merge into the Heart, and the true Self will shine forth."

— Sri Ramana Maharshi

Practical Steps:

  1. Notice the "I"-thought - Be aware of your sense of "I" or "myself"
  2. Turn inward - Instead of looking outward at objects, turn your attention backward to the source of the "I"-thought
  3. Abide in the "I"-thought - When the mind wanders, gently bring it back to the sense of "I"
  4. Let go - Eventually, even the "I"-thought will dissolve, revealing the true Self

Common Misunderstandings

❌ Self-Inquiry is NOT:

  • A mental exercise or intellectual analysis
  • Repeating "Who am I?" verbally
  • Trying to get an answer from the mind
  • A concentration practice on a particular object

✅ Self-Inquiry IS:

  • A turning of attention backward to the source
  • An investigation into the nature of the "I"
  • Abiding as the Self, not as the body-mind
  • The path of knowledge (Jnana Yoga) simplified

Relationship with Other Practices

Ramana Maharshi emphasized that Self-Inquiry is suitable for the mature seeker. However, he also acknowledged that other practices can prepare the mind:

Practice Purpose Relationship to Self-Inquiry
Self-Inquiry Direct path to Self-realization Primary practice
Surrender (Saranagati) Offering the ego to the Divine Complementary - both lead to the same goal
Devotion (Bhakti) Purification of the heart Prepares the mind for inquiry
Meditation (Dhyana) Concentration and focus Can be a stepping stone to inquiry

Selected Quotes

"Your duty is to be and not to be this or that. 'I am that I am' - sum up all in this, whereas to be this or that is works."

"The quest 'Who am I?' is the very essence of all mantras and the foremost of all works of devotion. If the mind is就是要turned inward in this way, all thoughts (including the 'I'-thought) will cease, and the Divine Self, which always exists, will shine forth of itself."

"When other thoughts arise, instead of allowing them to be, ask yourself: 'To whom do they arise?' This investigation will lead you back to the source - the 'I'-thought. Then, hold on to the 'I'-thought and investigate its nature."
