🙏 Sri Ramana Maharshi

Sri Ramana Maharshi (1879–1950)

The world-renowned sage, widely regarded as the greatest saint of modern times.

🙏

Birth: Dec 30, 1879, Tiruchuli, Tamil Nadu (Arudra Darshanam day)

Original Name: Venkataraman Iyer

Awakening: July 1896 (aged 16), in Madurai

Arrival at Arunachala: Sept 1, 1896, Tiruvannamalai

Mahanirvana: April 14, 1950, 8:47 PM, Sri Ramanasramam

Honorific: Bhagavan Sri Ramana Maharshi (Bhagavan = Lord/God)

Philosophy: Advaita Vedanta · Shaivism

👶 Early Life (1879–1896)

Venkataraman was born into an orthodox Brahmin family in Tiruchuli. His birth coincided with Arudra Darshanam—the day of Lord Shiva's darshan. In later years, he was widely seen as an incarnation of Dakshinamurti, the manifestation of Shiva who teaches in silence.

He was the second of four children. His father, Sundaram Iyer, was a pleader, and his mother, Alagammal, was a pious woman. As a child, Venkataraman was athletic and popular, nicknamed "Golden Hand" because his teams always won. He had a unique trait: an exceptionally deep sleep, so profound that his friends could strike his body without waking him.

In 1892, his father's sudden death at age 42 sparked his first thoughts on mortality. In 1895, hearing the name "Arunachala" from a relative triggered a massive spiritual resonance in his heart. Around the same time, reading the Periyapuranam—the lives of 63 Tamil Shaivite saints—deeply moved him, and he began praying at the Meenakshi temple for similar grace.

⚡ The Death Experience (1896)

In July 1896, at the age of 16, Venkataraman was suddenly seized by a violent, overwhelming "fear of death." Though he was in perfect health, he felt he was about to die. Instead of seeking help, he decided to investigate the matter himself:

  • He lay down like a corpse, stiffened his limbs, and held his breath.
  • He asked: "This body is dead... but with the death of the body, do I die? Is this body 'I'?"
  • He realized: The body is silent and lifeless, yet he felt the full force of his personality—a voice within.
  • The insight: "The body dies, but the Spirit transcends it. I am the deathless Spirit."

This was not a mere intellectual exercise but a living truth that flashed through him. The fear of death vanished forever, and his sense of "I" merged into the continuous flow of Self-awareness, which never left him thereafter.

"The fear of death had vanished once and for all. Absorption in the Self continued from that moment untinged by any other thought."
— Sri Ramana Maharshi

🚶 Departure for Arunachala (Sept 1, 1896)

After his awakening, Venkataraman lost interest in school and social life. His brother's constant rebukes finally acted as a catalyst. On August 29, 1896, he left home under the pretext of attending a special class, taking only 3 rupees and leaving 2 for his brother with a note:

"I have, in search of my Father and in obedience to His command, started from here. This is embarking on a virtuous enterprise. Therefore none need grieve over this affair. To trace this out, no money need be spent. Your college fee has not yet been paid. Two rupees are herewith."
— Venkataraman's farewell note

He reached Tiruvannamalai on September 1, 1896. He went straight to the sanctum of the Arunachaleswara Temple and embraced the Lingam in a state of ecstasy.

🏔️ The Arunachala Years (1896–1922)

The Temple Period (1896–1899)

He spent the first weeks in the thousand-pillared hall, then moved to the Patala Lingam cave, where he sat in such deep samadhi that he didn't notice insects biting his legs. Later, he moved to Pavalakkunru temple, where his mother finally found him. Despite her pleas to return home, he remained silent, eventually writing: "The Ordainer controls the fate of souls... What is destined not to happen will not happen... Silence is best."

Virupaksha Cave (1899–1916)

He lived in the Virupaksha Cave for 17 years. In 1902, Sivaprakasam Pillai asked him a series of questions about the Self, which became the core text Nan Yar? (Who am I?). In 1907, the great scholar Ganapati Muni recognized his greatness and gave him the name Sri Ramana Maharshi.

🕊️ Mahanirvana (1950)

In 1948, a cancerous growth appeared on his arm. Despite multiple surgeries, he refused radical treatment, saying: "Why are you so attached to this body? Let it go. Where can I go? I am here."

On the night of April 14, 1950, as devotees sang hymns to Arunachala, his breathing slowed and he peacefully passed away. At that exact moment (8:47 PM), a bright meteor was seen by many, including photographer Henri Cartier-Bresson, moving slowly across the sky toward the summit of Arunachala.

📖 Core Teachings

1. Self-Inquiry (Atma-Vichara): The most direct path. By asking "Who am I?" and tracing the source of the 'I'-thought, the ego dissolves into the Heart.

2. Surrender: The path of giving up the ego to the Divine. He taught that Self-Inquiry and Surrender are eventually the same.

3. Silence: His highest teaching. He often taught through silent presence, considering words a secondary form of instruction.
